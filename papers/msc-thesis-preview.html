DIFFERENTIAL VOLATILITY RESPONSES TO INFRASTRUCTURE AND REGULATORY EVENTS IN CRYPTOCURRENCY MARKETS

Full Monograph in Progress Year: 2025 Preview: 3 Chapters

MSc Finance Analytics - King's College London

Author: Murad Farzulla

This preview contains selected chapters from the full monograph, which is currently being written. The complete analysis includes additional sections on data analysis, results, discussion, and conclusions.

Abstract

This research examines differential volatility impacts between infrastructure and regulatory events on major cryptocurrencies using TARCH-X models with sentiment controls. Analyzing 50 classified events across six major cryptocurrencies (BTC, ETH, XRP, BNB, LTC, ADA) from 2019 to 2025, the study employs GDELT-based sentiment decomposition to isolate distinct information channels.

The methodology combines traditional event study approaches with advanced econometric modeling, incorporating continuous sentiment proxies alongside discrete event dummies to decompose volatility into baseline dynamics, sentiment-driven pressure, and discrete event shocks. This framework enables systematic testing of whether markets process operational and regulatory risks through fundamentally different mechanisms.

Literature Review

Theoretical Foundations: Market Efficiency and Information Processing in Digital Asset Markets

The theoretical foundation for understanding cryptocurrency market responses to different event types rests on the intersection of market microstructure theory, information economics, and behavioural finance. The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH), as formulated by Fama (1970), provides the baseline theoretical expectation that markets should rapidly incorporate all available information into asset prices. Under the strong-form EMH, both regulatory information and infrastructure disruptions would be immediately reflected in prices with no persistent abnormal volatility effects. However, the unique characteristics of cryptocurrency markets create conditions that may fundamentally violate EMH assumptions and enable differential processing of distinct information types (Liu and Tsyvinski, 2021).

The theoretical foundation for volatility modeling in financial markets originates with Engle's (1982) autoregressive conditional heteroscedasticity (ARCH) model, which first captured the time-varying nature of financial market volatility. This breakthrough enabled researchers to model volatility clustering. Bollerslev (1986) generalized this framework to the GARCH model, which has become the workhorse of volatility analysis in both traditional and cryptocurrency markets.

The Adaptive Markets Hypothesis (AMH) proposed by Lo (2004) offers a more nuanced framework for understanding time-varying market efficiency in cryptocurrency markets. Under the AMH, market efficiency is not a static property but evolves as market participants learn, adapt, and develop new trading technologies. This evolutionary perspective is particularly relevant for understanding how markets might process regulatory announcements differently from infrastructure failures, as participants develop distinct heuristics for each event type.

Methodology

Cryptocurrency Selection and Data

The selection of cryptocurrencies balanced statistical power, data quality, and market representativeness. Following Liu and Tsyvinski (2021) and Makarov and Schoar (2020), data integrity was prioritized over sample size. Selection criteria required: (i) continuous trading throughout January 2019 to August 2025, (ii) sustained top-decile liquidity, and (iii) distinct market archetypes to capture heterogeneous event responses.

The final sample comprises six cryptocurrencies: Bitcoin (BTC) as market baseline and systematic risk factor, Ethereum (ETH) capturing DeFi infrastructure exposure, XRP as regulatory case study given SEC litigation, Binance Coin (BNB) representing centralization risks, Litecoin (LTC) as control asset with high BTC correlation, and Cardano (ADA) as alternative proof-of-stake implementation.

Price and volume data are sourced from CoinGecko's institutional API, representing volume-weighted averages across major exchanges at 00:00:00 UTC. The 80-month study period encompasses complete market cycles including the 2020 to 2021 bull market, 2022 contagion crisis, and 2023 to 2025 regulatory normalization.

Event Classification Framework

From an initial corpus of 208 candidates, systematic filtering yielded 50 distinct events classified into two categories based on market mechanism:

  • Infrastructure events (n=27): Incidents affecting transaction or settlement mechanics including exchange outages, chain halts, protocol exploits, consensus changes, and halvings
  • Regulatory events (n=23): Legal or supervisory actions that alter the informational environment while preserving trading mechanics, including enforcement actions, ETF approvals, and legislative frameworks

GDELT-Based Sentiment Proxy

The methodology employs hierarchical keyword matching using GDELT's structured theme taxonomy to construct cryptocurrency sentiment indices. Raw tone scores are volume-weighted and normalized through recursive detrending via z-score transformation using 52-week rolling windows. Rather than calculating separate indices from disjoint article sets, normalized aggregate sentiment is decomposed by topical proportions to create regulatory and infrastructure components.

Volatility Modelling Framework

Three nested GARCH specifications examine cryptocurrency volatility dynamics: (1) GARCH(1,1) baseline capturing volatility clustering, (2) TARCH(1,1) introducing leverage parameter for asymmetric responses, and (3) TARCH-X incorporating continuous sentiment proxies and event dummy variables. This hierarchical approach enables systematic testing of asymmetric volatility responses, differential event impacts, and incremental explanatory power of continuous sentiment beyond discrete events.

Limitations

This study's findings are subject to several interconnected limitations spanning data measurement, methodological scope, and analytical choices that collectively constrain generalizability while informing future research directions.

Sentiment Measurement and Data Quality Constraints

GARCH model estimations revealed extremely high persistence parameters approaching or reaching unity (BTC: 1.000, XRP: 1.000, ETH: 0.996), indicating near-integrated or non-stationary variance processes. This suggests cryptocurrency volatility exhibits stronger persistence than can be adequately captured by standard GARCH specifications, potentially explaining the lack of statistical significance in event impact estimates. The high persistence effectively absorbs discrete event shocks into the long-memory volatility process, making individual event effects difficult to isolate.

The GDELT-based sentiment proxy exhibits multiple measurement limitations. GDELT's English-language bias potentially underrepresents sentiment from Asian markets that constitute significant cryptocurrency trading volumes, while dictionary-based tone scoring captures journalistic framing rather than market sentiment. Factual crisis reporting may register neutral tone while retrospective narratives can paradoxically generate positive scores, creating disconnects between media framing and market perception.

Weekly temporal aggregation, while reducing noise and computational costs, may obscure intraday sentiment dynamics crucial during rapidly evolving crises. Cryptocurrency markets operate continuously, yet significant sentiment shifts within weekly windows may be averaged away, reducing responsiveness to acute market stress.

Event Selection and Sample Limitations

The event study design's reliance on publicly verifiable documents may inadvertently exclude opaque technical incidents, particularly in decentralized networks with varying disclosure practices. The standardized event window ensures methodological consistency but may inadequately capture longer-term volatility persistence following major structural events, suggesting estimates may represent lower bounds on total volatility impact.

The six-cryptocurrency sample, while ensuring data quality and continuous trading history, limits generalizability to the broader digital asset ecosystem. Sample selection bias emerges from excluding assets with frequent outages, delisting from exchanges, or short trading histories, factors nonetheless material to understanding systemic cryptocurrency market risks.

Methodological Scope and Technical Constraints

Daily price data ensures consistency and liquidity filtering but may omit intraday volatility spikes affecting high-frequency markets. The intended implementation of OHLC-based volatility estimators was precluded by API rate limiting constraints, forcing reliance on close-to-close return calculations. Exploratory analysis of available intraday data revealed rapid decay patterns within hours of event announcements, suggesting daily frequency may understate adjustment speeds.

Advanced volatility modelling approaches including FIGARCH specifications for long-memory persistence and regime-switching models for structural breaks were explored in preliminary iterations but ultimately excluded. While theoretically advantageous for capturing cryptocurrency market dynamics, implementation proved computationally intensive and methodologically complex, with initial attempts encountering convergence issues.

Methodological Evolution

The final methodology reflects deliberate strategic choices prioritizing breadth over depth. Initial analysis employed extensive robustness validation and sophisticated outlier detection, but expanding scope to six cryptocurrencies across 50 events over 80 months necessitated streamlined approaches to maintain analytical tractability.

Research focus evolved toward examining exogenous event impacts through TARCH-X specifications with continuous sentiment variables and discrete event dummies. This choice prioritized the novel contribution of decomposed GDELT sentiment integration and differential event impact measurement over pure volatility asymmetry modelling. These methodological choices reflect deliberate research prioritization: comprehensive cross-asset, cross-event coverage with theoretically motivated exogenous variables was deemed more valuable than intensive single-asset validation.
